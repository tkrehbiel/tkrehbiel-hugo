We had a good opportunity to stretch our legs
there but the train finally pulled out. I was one of
the first to scramble aboard the car, so I got a
place on the floor to sleep. I got well under a bench,
thinking that there noone would step on me. But
I miscalculated, for a fellow was sleeping on(?) the
bench above me and just as I was dropping off
to sleep. his foot dropped down onto my ear. I jumped
and banged my head on the bench above me. So it
was quite a while after I got to sleep. But I
really did sleep, I’ve long ago gotten over my in-
tolerance of a strange bed.

They dont sound reveille on the cars, but force
of habit got us all out at sunrise. We had a break-
fast of hardtack and corned beef and tomatoes, straight(?)
from the can. And I’ll say right here that that
was what we lived on for three days. Tomato cans
were rolling all around the floor all the time. The
second night I even slept on one for a pillow. But
they were just what we needed, for water was scarce,
and the juice went good with our hardtack.

The country had changed during the night,
and changed for the better. The fields were larger
and the mud fences were lacking. In their place
were neat hedges. Vinyards [sic] were now plentiful. But
they dont look like our vinyards [sic], the vines are planted
about four feet apart each way and trained on a pole.
The vine is small, with a small yellow leaf. The
grapes though are in large, long bunches. They will
not be ripe for a month or so yet; I investigated.
We passed some fine pasture lands, with herds of
heavy white cattle grazing. There were not many
sheep. Fruit trees seemed entirely lacking.
