But we are drilling right along.
They have added 45 minutes to our
sleep period; we get up at 5.45 instead of
5.00. Still, the nights are now so cold
that we cant get any sleep at all. I
was under three blankets last night, but
I couldn’t sleep for shivering. Tonight
I’ll spread my overcoat over me yet. Then
as a last resort I have the shelter half
and slicker. After that it will be a matter
of crawling under a strawpile. They say
the winters here are very severe and every-
thing looks that way.

Crops here are all in, and the farmers
are doing their fall plowing. In this ground
it would be hard enough with a real plow
but you ought to see the plowing they do
with their plows. The furrows are about three
inches deep and six inches wide, but it is
impossible to distinguish them. The ground
is so dry that it breaks up in heavy chunks
and that’s the way it lays. I certainly
cant understand how anything grows in it.
I had charge of a party digging a garbage

[Continues facing page.]

hole. For the first two feet the ground was
so dry that it broke into little dice(?), as hard
as brick. Below that it was stiff, and
as sticky as glue. It would make fine
brick, but that is all that it is good for.

The fruit here is about all upened(?) now.
We can get grapes, buy them I mean, they
dont grow much around here. They are
certainly very inferior to our own. Apples
here are worthless. About the only fruit
they have here is plums. They are blue,
about two inches long and an inch in
diameter, and very full of worms. But
when the wormy(?) parts are cut away, the
remainder makes good eating. We dont
have to buy them, they grow around the
country like the chinquapin do at
home. The French call them prunes(?),
and it may be that they are the
real prunes. Blackberries too are plen-
tiful, but they all grow alongside the
roads, hence are crusted deep with white
dust. They taste rather like mulberries.

Nuts too are ripening fast. Hazel-
nuts are about ripe, and walnuts
will be ready and in about three weeks.
