Four oclock came and no train, in place
of it, news that we would probably be there till
12.30. So we tried to make ourselves com-
fortable. We built a fire and while I was rustling(?)
wood for it, I saw one example of how the war
has hit France. On the hillside above us were
little scattered patches of vinyard [sic], none larger
than 50 feet square. Round about them I found
lots of sticks, about 3 feet long. I began to won-
der how they got there and when I examined
closely I saw that the whole hillside had once
been a vinyard [sic]; I was picking up the stakes from
vines that had been choked out by weeds and
brush.

At about 10 oclock they brought us word that
we would be there all night. A party of us
promptly slipped off into town, to hunt up
a dry place to sleep. We found a barnful of
hay, and crawled in. The owner came in in
a few minutes and talked to us, but we couldn’t
understand him, so we lay where we were, and
he eventually got tired and left. We had
a mighty good sleep.
