We opened both doors of our Pullman and arranged
the benches so we could see out, then we got into po-
sition to enjoy the scenery. For a long time this
consisted of a high cutbank(?) and a lot of tincans.
The route of the trooptrains can be traced all over
France by the trail of tincans and bisquit [sic] boxes. Speak-
of bisquit [sic], we made the acquaintance of hardtack
on this train; got every intimate with it, I might say.
It is a small biscuit about two inches square and a
quarter inch thick. But it certainly tastes good; we
chewed on them continually. Personally I would
prefer them a little soltier and sweeter, but I’ll let
it pass.

We finally got up on the level and our train put
on an almost respectable burst of speed. The country
was just as I described around the port. It was
interesting, but not enough so to keep me awake,
so I spread my raincoat on the floor, rolled up
someone else’s for a pillow and went to sleep.

I slept about two hours, then got up and let some-
one else have my bed. The country was still much
the same, though the fields were getting a little larger.
The principle crops seemed to be small grains,
wheat and oat fields were plentiful, the grain not
yet cut. I didn’t see any wheat as good as our’s,
didn’t see it in all France.

Our train kept rolling on in a very half hearted
way. We would go a few miles, kilometers, I should
say; then stop. We decided that they didn’t need
us very badly at the front. At these stops the
kids came running to the train, begging for
souveniers [sic] and bisquits [sic] and cigaretts [sic]. It seems
that tobacco is very scarce in France, all the boys
beg cigaretts [sic] from the soldiers. A couple even rowed
out to our ship, as we lay at anchor in the harbor,
to beg them. At first they got plenty, then the fellows
tightened up and at last they gave out nothing but
