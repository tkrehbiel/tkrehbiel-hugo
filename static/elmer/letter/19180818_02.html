[Another two-page affair.]

The trees were not crowded together, but
were far enough apart for the grass to grow
well between them. I suspect that it was
considered more as a sheep pasture than a
forest.

I remember reading, in a book about
China, of rice fields that could be covered by
a table cloth. I was constantly reminded of
it on this walk. Wherever the slope was
such that it could be plowed at all, were tiny
little fields. I suppose they averaged about
a quarter of an acre in extent; I didn’t see
any all evening larger than an acre. On them
grains were growing, or sometimes alfalfa. On
fields facing south, were vinyards [sic]. The vines
were planted about three feet apart each way.
They did not seem to run at all, they only
grew into small bushes. The grapes were in
large bunches, a dozen or so to a vine, but
they were tiny little things, not much larger
than the chicken grapes that grow wild at
home. The cultivation of the vinyards [sic] must
be all by hand, for the slopes were mostly
too steep for horses. Besides, some of them
had garden truck(?) growing between the rows.

[Research note: “Garden truck” is an old term for market vegetables.]

We see evidence of this economy of
cultivated ground all about. The farmers
build house, barn and everything else in
one continuous building about a paved courtyard
about thirty feet square. On the outside the

crops come right to the walls of the build-
ing, and perhaps there is a row of grapevine
trained against the side of the house yet. There
are no flowergardens. Sometimes there are potted
plants setting at the doorway, but the only
flower garden I’ve seen yet was one about five
feet square. Fifty seven varieties of flowers were
growing in that.

The sides of this cart track were lined with
blackberry bushes, pretty well loaded with fruit.
It was just beginning to ripen, but if those
I tasted are fair samples of French blackberries
I’ll have to put another black mark against
France. But they might make good pies, so
next week I’m going out and gather a canful
and see [–] if the cook will let me bake a
pie. I dont think I’ve forgotten how. There
are apple trees along the main roads here, but
they are not ripe yet. Gooseberries, too, grow
wild here, but they are pretty well all gone now.
I got many a stomach ache from them while
they lasted.

I am writing this leaning back against
a stone wall in a field just out of the village.
It is almost getting too dark to write. In
