There were openings in the bushes on those mud
fences but we managed to miss all of them
and had to scramble through anywhere; we walked
into a swamp halfway up to our knees; and the
posts were so hidden that we walked by many
of them and had to hunt them up later. Then
when we did get back to the guardhouse, or guard
tent, it was, there was not room for us inside and
we could only stand around in the rain. We didn’t
get any sleep all night.

When daylight came the camp was inches
deep in mud, the rain still coming down. We
were the worst disgusted lot of men in France
and I for one heartily wished I was in the navy(?).

We were relieved from guard at four o’clock and
got to our company in time for the order to make
packs. We were to leave our shelter tent and one
blanket out, so we could sleep, but that order was
soon changed and we packed up ready to move.
I remembered my last pack and so was very
careful. As a result I got a pack that rode beau-
tifully, I could have hiked several hours with it.
Most of my stuff was wet though, so it was aw-
fully heavy. I was afraid too, that my things would
get moldy.

After we had our packs made up we stood around
as usual. The night began to get cold, with a
strong wind. It was impossible to lie down, so
we just had to stand around and shiver. The
cooks finally built up a big fire and we all got
around it in a ring, and so we passed most of
the night.

It was about three oclock in the morning
that we got orders to sling packs and fall in.
