We went through two large cities in the afternoon.
Even here the architecture is of the same sort, and
they look just as old. They are certainly neater
than American cities though; in place of our
lots and back yards piled high with rubbish they
have flower and truck gardens. I noticed trolley
tracks in the streets, but cars run at very infrequent
intervals. There did not seem to be any parks in
the cities but this is not surprising, for the country
round about is so well groomed as any of our parks.

On that first day too we passed a ruined
castle. These things all are familiar to me from
photographs but still they are interesting. This one
was on a craggy rock, not more than 100 feet
above the surface of the country. Only a few walls
were standing, and these were thick and grey and
covered with ivy, all according to regulation.

Late in the evening we pulled up in a fairly large
city. Here the train stopped and we piled out for hot
coffee. A Frenchman brought it down the line in big
pots and I honestly thought he was going to oil the
train. It looked exactly like heavy brown oil. We
have very little milk in the army but they do give
it to us in our coffee, so I am not used to black
coffee. This coffee had no milk or sugar in it, but
all the same it was exactly what we needed and
