In place of the mud fences they had where we landed,
these people have four foot thick walls of chalk rock.
I suppose, though, that is just to get the rock off
the fields. The roads are also metallized(?) with this
rock, and the motor truck that run over them
look as if they had been driven through flour.

There is a church in this village that might
have been built before Christ, if such a thing were
possible. It does have a stone in the floor, pre-
sumably a gravestone, dated 1553(?). There is nothing
notable about its architecture, except that like all
French buildings it is very short of window space.
And it is roofed with slabs of chalk rock, for this
can be split almost like slate. All the buildings
here are roofed this way, the stones laid to a
depth of a foot. And the roof over our head sags(?)
ominously. There is one house up the street farther
on which has caved in. No attempt has been made to
repair it; I suppose it would be easier to put
up a new house in a new place than remove all
that rock.

There are very few people left in the village.
We dont miss them either; they all look foreign, even
the children. As for the famous French chicken, there
aint no such animal. But soldiers who have visited(?)
[–] the cities say that they really do exist.
So I have something to look forward to. But even
so, the only chicken I want to see is the Statue
of Liberty.

The country round about is a genuine farm-

ing country; no crazy-quilt pattern of dinky
little fields, such as I described in my last letter.
In the fields between the villages there are no fences,
and no houses either. The French farmer lives in
a village and drives to his fields every morning.
The principal crop here seems to be wheat and some
other small grains; oats, barley, etc. Potatoes are
plentiful. The soil is a mostly red clay, plentifully
mixed with the chalk rock, a pretty bad mess to
work in I would think. And the hills are just as
plentiful as they are in Virginia. There are patches
of woods scattered about, and quite a forest on the
skyline. Altogether, if I were well fed, and happy
and in good humor I would call it a very beautiful
country. As it is, I wouldn’t trade an acre of Virginia
for a whole county(?) in France. I will add that I cant
see while why they call it “Sunny France.” It
rains, drizzles, or sprinkles, or squirts, about three
times a week and even when the sun does shine
it has not half the brilliance that it has at home.

But there is one thing that I envy the French,
and that is their roads. I certainly hope I will
live to see America so well off in that respect.
They run everywhere, branching out every mile or
so and every one, even little cart tracks across
the fields, is beautifully macadamized. They
look like white ribbons. They are bordered with trees,
usually English walnuts, and in some cases, fruit
trees. But fruit trees, in this country, look as
miserable and out of place as we feel.

people; that is the live stock. This seems to
