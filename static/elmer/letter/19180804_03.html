There are very few sheep and I’ve seen only three
hogs in all France. Those were very tall, lean, slab(?)-
sided animals, at least two and half feet high
at the shoulders.

The farm marchinery here is all American,
I mean the mowers and harvesters and such tools.
There are McCormick and Wood binders, and
McCormick mowers and Deere plows and various
other things like that to make me homesick. But
you ought to see a French plow. The beam is
about ten feet long, just a round pole. It rides
up on two wheels, the two being hitched together by
some sort of chain arrangement. I would like to
try one. The Share, I might add, is about the
size of your hand. A Frenchman always hitches
his horses tandem, no matter what he is doing, so
the wagons here, even very large ones, are fitted
with shafts. It looks rather queer. But the
queerest thing of all is the brake. It is operated
from the rear end by a crank! They are fitted
with one thing, though, that I consider a good idea.
They have a small windlass(?) on the back end, and
when a load of wheat, or hay is carried, a rope
is passed over the top and tightened down by
means of this windlass. I might add that all
this machinery stands out in the open, just as
in the good old U.S.A. But then, they’ve only had such things for the last forty years; they
havn’t [sic] had time, yet, to put up sheds for them.

[Continues on facing page. Top of page slightly cut off.]

That is enough about the French people; now
for something about ourselves. I often think how
disgusted we were at Camp Meade and when I think
of it I want to be kicked. We were in Heaven there.
“Over here” we get up at 5 oclock, and five oclock, in
this section, is a cold time of the day. We can often
see our breath, just as on a winter day at home. We
fall out for reveille and fifteen minutes setting
up exercises. Then we go immediately for mess.
After mess we have about half an hour, to clean
our billet and roll up our beds. Then we fall out
for inspection. This takes fifteen or twenty min-
utes. A few minutes later we fall in and march
out to our drill field, march over two or three
hills, onto the roughest piece of ground, or rather
loose rock, in the country. There we drill from
seven oclock till twelve, and believe me, we DRILL.
Two rests in those five hours is unusual; we have
gone on without any. Then we have an hour off
for mess. We used to come in after it, then go out
again, but lately they have been bunging it out
to us. When we did come in, that time was taken
off the hour, not off the drill period. After mess
we drill till four oclock. Then we come in and
have an hour off to clean our guns. As I said, they
are inspected every day, and in this climate,
they rust(?) every day. Mess is at 5.45 and retreat
at 6.45. Taps is at 9.00, but all it does is wake
us up; we are in bed long before that.

That is just a plain, bald statement of
fact. I wish I could give you an idea of how
tired we are at the end of such a perfect day.
