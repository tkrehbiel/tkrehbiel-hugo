last, of course, but I’m sure that hill is a mile
long, and as steep as the hill in front of our house.

We had a short rest on top, then struck out
into the country. Soldiers were camped to right
and left, on all available space, as far as we
could see. We had to go out about two miles be-
fore we found a place. Then we pulled up in what
had once been a pasture, but which was now an
expanse of greasy, black mud. We pitched our
shelter tents and tried to make ourselves comfortable.
Also we began to wish we were back on the ship.

The country hereabouts certainly looked
foreign. It was all divided up into tiny little
fields, none of them larger than four acres. For
fences they had mounds of earth, built up with
straight sides to a height of about six feet and
overgrown with bushes. Some of them looked rather
neat, but mostly they were as bad as the fence-
row between us and Ladne(?). They gave the country
a very mangly [sic] appearance. The soil was black
