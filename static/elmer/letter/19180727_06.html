Wood here is very scarce. We see constant
evidence of that. The landscape is dotted plentifully
with trees and all are trimmed in a curious way.
The larger branches are all cut off, all the way up
the trunk, leaving only a feathery foliage up the trunk
and a little tuft of branches on top. Some species
of trees are cut always at the same places, so they have
heavy, nobby [sic] trunks and only a fine, bushy ball of
foliage. When a tree is cut down here the branches
and brush are all saved for firewood, [–] even the
larger roots are grubbed out.

Water, too is scarce, in spite of the fact that
it rains every day. Our water was brought out by
motor truck. So all we had was about two canteen
fuls a day. We had to wash and shave and
brush our teeth with that allowance.

We had scarcly gotten our tents up when our
company was ordered to put out a guard. Of
course I was in it. We established a guard of
31 posts, all around the camp. To post it, we had
to climb over about 10 of those mud fences, jump
a mushy(?) pool and walk a few hundred feet over
a peat bog. It was about a mile walk in all,
and took about half an hour to post a guard in
daytime. We were standing two hours on and four
off.
