find the joints in the rocks; then prying

But when that position [–] is finally fin-
ished, I’ll bet all the Germans in captivity
couldn’t capture it. It is on the top of a
very steep hill, about three hundred feet or
more high, steep as the roof of our house.
The trenches are not, as I always thought, ex-
actly on the crest, but back about 200 feet, giv[ing?]
a great sweep of fire. And at the foot of the
hill the ground is level and bare, with no
place short of five miles where an enemy could
place heavy artillery. I hope we get such
a position to hold when we go to the front.

From the top of that hill, too, we got
a good view of the valley below, and I
almost call France beautiful. The fields are
not, as with us, square, but irregular or
curving, making a more interesting pattern.
The ground was flat, or rather, it looked flat
from up there, and it all looked so tame and

It is just harvest time here now. The
wheat is nearly all cut and the oats is just
ready. These French people have had the
nerve to try to improve on an American
farm machine. They take a mower(?) and fasten
slats of wood to the frame that carries the
blade, in such a manner that the slats
lie flat on the ground when the machine is
in operation. Then they put another seat
in front of and to the right of, the driver’s
seat. Someone, a woman or young boy, sits
on this seat with a contrivance like a rake,
only the handle is on the same plane with
the teeth, and set off from the perpendicular,
so - [drawing of a rake-like tool] Then, when the mower is
operating, the grain is caught on these slats
and raked off when a bundle accumulates.
