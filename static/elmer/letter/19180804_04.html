But that isn’t the worst, by any means.
Our major made us a speech, just before we left.
He said that our mess over here wouldn’t be very
good. Believe me, he said it. For breakfast, we
have three or four slices of bacon, and a slice of
bread and cup of coffee, very rarely anything else.
For dinner we have a beef stew and slice of bread
and coffee, or a small steak and something besides,
a spoonful of corn, or peas, or rice, or something
like that. For supper we have beans. And we never
have enough. I dont believe I’ve eaten six meals
in France when I really felt satisfied. And you
cant buy anything outside, there is nothing to buy.
We are not permitted to buy milk and eggs. Some-
times we can get a little brick of cheese, for about
30 cents, but outside that we can just grin and
bear it. You understand, of course, that it is
not the fault of the cooks, or mess sergeant or
mess officer. They are just as sore as we are. But
I wish the one that is responsible would happen
around here. We would show him that what Sher-
man said was right*. The other day our general
was around to inspect us and I heard him re-
mark, “This is a thin looking lot of men, dont
they get enough to eat?” They dont. So when you
are eating sweet corn, and new potatoes and snap
beans, and hot cornbread and butter and jelly and
milk and eggs, and nice fat pies, you think of
me and my three slices of bacon, because that’s

[Continues facing page. The top is slightly cut off.]

when I think of you. I think I could forgive
a good many things if I could get sweets, but
we simply cant. Today, Sunday, we each got
a spoonful of peach jam, the only sweets I’ve
had in France. And the future looks just as
empty. I’m beginning to hope I’ll get wounded;
they treat you nice in the hospitals. At one of
our stops across France, the Red Cross served us
each a cup of coffee, and I’ll remember it as the
only cup of good coffee I got while in the army.

We are quite a ways from the front, though
we are in a Department that has figured frequently
in the war news. And yet we dont hear a thing
about the war. We have had only one piece of news
that I consider authentic. The captain of the
company that was billeted in this village just
before us came back from the front to look after some
stuff and he told one of our officers that he lost
40 men out of his company in one day from gas.
So I’m going to get on very intimate terms with my
mask. I’ve got a good on [sic], and I mean to keep it
good.

We get little drippings of news, or rumors and
they appear to favor the Allies. So we are in good
spirits, and some even have hopes of eating
their Christmas dinner at home. I wish them luck.
We will move up toward the front very shortly, pro-
bably. I might say certainly, before you get this
letter. We wont go to the fighting; just close enough
to hear the guns and get a whiff of gas once in
a while. Well, we are ready, and as I said, we
are in a mood to send a few Germans to a worse
place than they got us into.
