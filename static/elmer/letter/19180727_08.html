all day and the roads were inches deep with
greasy black mud. Every few minutes someone
would go down with a loud “Whop” and an awful
curse. I started to laugh, and couldn’t stop. I
myself walked in the wagon rut; there I was
ankle deep in mud, but my feet didn’t have
room to slide.

After we got out on the highroad it was not
so bad. The footing was good and as I said,
my pack rode well. The walk too was mostly
downhill. The thought that we were leaving that
vicinity made us almost cheerful, and when the
sun finally came out we felt happy once again.
But I certainly felt the loss of sleep, for with the
exception of four hours on the ship I had not slept
for three nights.

We got down to the railroad, where our train was
waiting for us. That train may be classed with the other
horrors of war. It was all freight cars, and French
freight cars at that. They are about fifteen or sixteen
feet long and half as wide; there are only four wheels.
Ours was fitted with eight benches. Forty men were
crowded into each car, and then the packs took
up half the room. We drew rations for three days,
and privately I thought that three days ought
to see us well on into Russia.

We pulled out at last and we all gave a final
yell when we left that port. We knew we couldn’t
get into anything worse. Our train crept along
very slowly, and it jolted and jarred worse than
the Southern. In America we have splendid roll-
ing stock(?) and rather indifferent roadbeds, in
France they have fine roadbeds and punk rolling
stock. The French system jolts the worst, but
perhaps I’m judging them too harshly, for they
have had very little attention since the war started.
