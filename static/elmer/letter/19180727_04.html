The town looked as old as the hills. The
houses were all of the same grey granite, laid in
rough courses. They are of were the square, unorna-
mented, high pitched roof sort that we have be-
come so familiar with in the photographs. The
streets were of coarse [sic] macadem [sic]. Everything looked
old; even the arched passages under the rail-
road embankment seemed to be centuries old.
It was a sort of shock to come across a new
building of reinforced concrete in course of
construction. And we came to a trolley track. The
rails were about 30” apart and were about like
the toys they sell at Christmas. We didn’t see
any of the cars though.

The inhabitants, we didn’t see very many,
didn’t look so outlandish as I had expected.
The women wore plain, dark colored dresses, that
were long enough to drag on the ground. They
wore little white caps, much like the caps
that Dunkards(?) wear. The men wore very plain
and patched up and dirty clothes, wholly modern.
But over it all they wore a long smock, that
came about to the knees. The little [–]
boys and girls dressed much alike, but the boys
wore trousers under their dresses. We saw a good
many wooden shoes too; I dont see how they
keep them on their feet.

[Research note: “Dunkards” is an old nickname for members of the Church of the Brethren.]

As I said; we started off in grand style.
The road wound around, so we couldn’t see
very far ahead and we were soon looking
ahead for the top of the hill. My pack bothered
me; it was about the size and shape of one
of those two bushel sacks that we get grass seed
in. I couldn’t get it to ride high, so it hung
