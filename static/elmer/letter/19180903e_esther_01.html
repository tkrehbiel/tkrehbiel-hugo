[Note that there are far fewer letters to Elmer than there are from Elmer. Perhaps Elmer didn’t or couldn’t save letters from home, especially during the time he was hospitalized, or many of them got lost in the unreliable WW1 mail. There isn’t much in the letters from home of interest to WW1 enthusiasts; it’s mainly of interest to Krehbiel genealogists.]

[This is a typed letter from Esther to Elmer. Esther is about a month from turning 18 on this date.]

Central Statistical Bureau,
116 New Interior Building
18th & E Sts., N. W.,
Washington, D. C.
Sept. 3, 1918.

Dear Bigs:

Yes, that’s where I work now, and I hearily [sic] wish I was safely
out of the Government too. This is the most awful place I ever was at.
Swell English. They dont [sic] give me anything to do all day long but about
three letters, I’ve finished them for today, and Mr. McDermott says may-
be there will be something to do later. In the mean time, kill time
as best you can. Soft job, boss over nothing. Get $1080 per annum
though. For all that, it is a small wonder that the government clerks
become old maids, cranks etc. There are twelve other stenographers
in this division, and every one of them is past thirty, and two of them
wont even see fifty again. They are mostly out of town people, and as
stupid as they look.

A bunch of us went on a picnic yesterday, Labor Day, gee if
we didnt [sic] have a swell time. There were thirteen pieces of chicken,
bought a dozen bananas, the old dago gave us thirteen, had some life
savers, counted them, and they were thirteen, had thirteen pairs,
I mean pares, no I dont [sic], I mean pears. Anyway, I mean the fruit, that
grows on trees. Everyth ing [sic] went in thirteens like that, and we had
a time in accordance with the number. I wish I had stayed down on the
farm (?). [sic]

Jake left for Camp Meade this morning at 7:30. Walter
registers on September 12th. This registration takes from 18 to 45.
There surely will be a bunch go in this one too.

I wrote a letter to President Wilson Saturday, and this morn-
ing he came in to see me, or rather he was here when I got in this
morning. I’m right in with the High guys now.

There are two fellows from Moundridge stationed at Camp
Humphreys, Accotink, Va. and ma has invited them up. Also there is
a A. R. C. Nurse at Camp Lee from Moundridge who she knows, and we
invited her up also, but she wrote that she is only allowed two weeks
out of six months, and if needed at the Hospital, she can not even get
them, so does not expect to get released for some time. She says she
likes it, but thinks easterners are funny. I think the same of the
people in this room, all westerners, and all extremely funny.

We can not send things over to you, but can you send us
things. You might send us some souvenirs. That is if you are not
broke.

Cant think of any thing more to write, and it is lunch
time anyway, so will go out and blow in some of the spondulics [sic] or
however you spell it.

Your sister,
Esther [written in pencil]
