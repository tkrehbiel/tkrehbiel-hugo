a bad place to live in. It is about 8 by
16 feet floor space, and 7 feet high, the walls
very solidly timbered, the roof of heavy corru-
gated iron, with rocks and earth piled over
it five feet deep. There is a tarpaper roof
over it all, so the dugout is dry, in spite
of the continual rain we have been having.
Six men are quartered in it, and we just
comfortably fill it. There is a double tier of
bunks at one end, at the other is a single
bunk, which I grabbed. It is an oblong box
about 7 x 2 1/2 feet, with sides 6” high, the bottom
closed by chicken wire. The whole is raised
off the floor on legs two feet high. On the
whole it is the best bunk I’ve had in France,
and if this woods ever dries off I’ll fill
the box with leaves and then I’ll be in heaven.
I want to be as comfortable as possible, for all
I do is lay around. Just at the foot of my
bunk is a small shelf fastened to the wall.
I sit on the foot end of the bunk and use this
shelf for a table, just now I am using it
as a writing desk. There is also a fireplace
here, and the a previous occupant filled up the
space under my bunk with dry wood. We
also have plenty of candles here so there is
no earthy [sic] complaint to make about these
