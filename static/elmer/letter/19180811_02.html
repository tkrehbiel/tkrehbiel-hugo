us would probably have jumped into the
river before now. But the first six or
seven months at the front, they tell me, are
spent on fatigue work, carrying supplies,
building roads, etc. If that is so, the
only way it would differ from here is that
we would have to slide into our gas masks
occasionally. But we’re getting used to
that, for several times a day we have
a gas alarm sprung on us, and we have
to get into our masks.

I suppose there will come a day
when I’ll love and honor that gasmask,
but today I lay awake at night to hate
it. I can get it on pretty snapily [sic] now,
but when it is on it is almost intolerable.
It seems to filter the oxygen out of the
air more than anything else. You suck
in wind till your diaphragm aches, yet
you dont feel satisfied. And the goggles
give only a very narrow range of vision
and you cant turn your head very
far on account of the traechea [sic] tube. It’s

[Continues facing page.]

a great invention.

We can now get passes over Sun-
days so there is one pleasant break in
the week. Today I visited a nearby town.
It was a pleasant little village and it
almost made me think I have judged
France too hastily. The houses were neat,
all heavily constructed of stone, of course,
the streets were clean and hard and
well kept, there were fountains here and
there, with a few heavy shade trees
about, and a creek flowing through the
center of it all. I went to church there, at
the Y.M.C.A. and I thought more of home
there in an hour than I usually do
in a day. None of us ever(?) realized till
now what a great place America is.

We have another diversion here. At
night we usually get a pretty good sup-
per. Then we sit around on the bridge and
