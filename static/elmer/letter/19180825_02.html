here is valuable; precious, I might say, so
the French people wouldn’t think of letting us
have a nice level field to tramp over. They
gave us a piece tilted about 30°, and littered
up with clumps of trees, and bushes, and
quarry holes and other such obstacles. It is
a fine piece for sham battles, though, because
it is easy to slide up under cover. I hope
our section of the front will be so easy to
defend.

In spite of such rough work, or perhaps
because of it, very few of the company fall out.
We have two men out of the 257 sick in
hospital from dysentery, but the others are
only getting tougher. We had a hike yesterday
morning with full packs, about 60 pounds. We
went about 7 miles and noone fell out. But
much as I admire these roads, I must say
they are hard on the feet. Our shoes are made
of half inch thick leather, and reinforced
with half pound of iron, so they are just about
as inflexible(?) as the wooden shoes these peas-
ants wear. My feet ache continually(?) but the
cold water in the creek here is the best thing

tonic in the world for them, so I am still
holding on. I certainly would rather hike than
drill on that hillside.

We are today getting rid of some of our
excess equipment, so it looks as if we were [sic]
moving out soon. Every move we have made
so far has been from bad to worse, I suppose
this next one will be just the same. But more
of us care very much. The Americans at the
front seem to be doing great work now, we would
be perfectly willing to join them and help end
this mess. For we certainly have learned more
over here in one month than we did at home
in three. We will soon be fit for the front.

I have just received a pass, so I’ll go
mosey around the country and see if I cant
get material enough to finish this letter.
I’ll just mention in passing; the first book
of my diary is now filled, I wish I could send
it to you. But I know I cant, for it is cram-
med full of forbidden matter. Also, I would
like to kill the man who said we could not take
cameras with us. This would be the most inter-
esting thing in the world twenty or thirty
years from now, yet we are not permitted to
record it.
