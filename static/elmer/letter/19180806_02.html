I forgot to mention that we had a creek
through the village. It is not large enough
for canoeing, but there are some fair swim-
ming holes in it. But oh! the water is cold.
Its colder in July than it is at home in
October. There is a reservoir at a nearby
town where we go swimming when we are not
too tired to walk there and back. That is
much better swimming than the creek, but
the bottom is too far down to be of any use
when you want to rest. One man from our
regiment was drowned there the first day we
got here; he was taken with cramps and the
others could not find him in time to pull
him out alive. Another man was drowned in
a canal near here.

Speaking of water leads(?) naturally
to the weather, for that is mostly water. At
our port of landing it rained daily; not an
honest-to-goodness rain, but a miserable driz-
zle that kept us hunched up and shivering.
At one of the stations we got out for hot coffee.
The sky was practically clear, yet within a
few minutes we had to run for our slickers.
I asked a Military Policeman there how often

[Continues facing page.]

it rained. He said, “I’ve been here two
days and its rained six times already.”
At this village we have about two rains a
week. They generally come during our drill
periods, because they dont let us come in
as they used to at Camp Meade, but tonight,
for variety, one is coming from the west and
flooding my bed. We never have thunderstorms.

I must add, though, that this country
would make a good summer resort. We sleep
under three blankets and they come within an
inch of being not enough. The first night I
came here I thought, “Oh joy! I can undress once
again and get some real sleep.” So I spread
one blanket on the floor and wrapped up in the
other. About half an hour later I got up and
put on a sweater and a pair of heavy socks.
About midnight I woke up and drew on my breeches.
A little later I put on my coat and before daylight
I had my overcoat spead [sic] out on top of me. Last
night I was on guard, and I had to put on my
overcoat under my rain coat.

In my last letter I had a good deal
to say about our rations. I take back most of
it. We were then on field rations; we are now on
garrison rations, and it is a vast improvement.
