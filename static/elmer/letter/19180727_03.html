there weren’t any docks. We came to anchor in
the bay. It was a fine harbor, but the thanks
were to nature, for there was little sign that
man had done much to improve it.

We had to spend another night on the ship.
That first afternoon was all taken up with business
among the ship’s officers. The harbor was full of
little motorboats, taking them back and forth.

In the morning we were routed out at four
oclock. We got our breakfast, then made up our packs
and came up on deck. At 7.15(?) we left the ship
and got onto the lighter that was to take us a-
shore and at 8.30 we got onto land once again.
There was no dock; we stepped out onto a section
of sea-wall. This wall was of rough granite
blocks, well laid up and fit to last a good
many years. All the same, I think the French
ought to lend a commission(?) to New York to learn
something about harbors and docks.

We stood around for an hour or so (this
is always understood, so I wont mention it again:
They give you about 10 minutes to make up a pack
and two seconds to get it on; then you stand around
for an hour) then marched on through the town.
The road was uphill, very much uphill. But
we had had a long rest, and had been stuffing
with good grub three times a day, so we felt
